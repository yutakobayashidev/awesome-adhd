# Towards Accessible Remote Work: Understanding Work-from-Home Practices of Neurodivergent Professionals

Source extracted from ACM DOI page on 2026-07-23. Full text was not fully available through extraction; abstract and bibliographic context were captured.

## Bibliographic facts

- Authors: Maitraye Das, John Tang, Kathryn E. Ringland, Anne Marie Piper.
- Venue: Proceedings of the ACM on Human-Computer Interaction, CSCW1, 2021.
- DOI: 10.1145/3449282.
- Method: interviews with 36 US-based neurodivergent professionals working from home during the COVID-19 pandemic.
- Conditions included Autism Spectrum Disorder, ADHD, learning disabilities, anxiety, depression, and related psychosocial disabilities.

## Captured abstract

Working from home became mainstream during COVID-19, but little was known about how disabled people engage in remote work from home or what access means there. The study interviewed 36 neurodivergent professionals. Findings: while working from home, they created accessible physical and digital workspaces, negotiated accessible communication practices, and balanced productivity and wellbeing. The paper reconsiders access in remote work and offers practical insights for inclusive work practices and remote collaboration tools.

## Relevance for async / synchronous work

The study is important because it treats remote work not as automatically accessible, but as a negotiated arrangement. For Toymaker/OpenBrief, this means asynchronous channels, meeting records, and status signals should not be framed as universal fixes. They become accessible only when people can negotiate communication practices, manage sensory and attention load, and protect wellbeing.
